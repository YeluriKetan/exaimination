Chapter 2: Cell Structure and Function

Cells are the basic unit of life. They are responsible for carrying out all of the essential functions necessary for an organism to survive. In this chapter, we will explore the structure and function of cells, from their membranes to their organelles.

Cell Membrane
The cell membrane, also known as the plasma membrane, is a thin, flexible barrier that surrounds the cell. It separates the internal environment of the cell from the external environment. The cell membrane is composed of a phospholipid bilayer, with proteins and carbohydrates embedded within it. This structure allows the cell to regulate what enters and leaves the cell, maintaining homeostasis.

Cytoplasm
The cytoplasm is the gel-like substance that fills the cell. It is made up of mostly water and contains various organelles and molecules necessary for cell function. Within the cytoplasm, there are also specialized structures called cytoskeletons that help maintain the cell's shape and assist in cell movement.

Nucleus
The nucleus is often referred to as the control center of the cell. It contains the cell's genetic material, DNA, which carries instructions for the cell's activities. The nucleus is surrounded by a double membrane called the nuclear envelope, which has pores that allow for the transport of molecules in and out of the nucleus.

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
The endoplasmic reticulum is a network of interconnected membranes that extends from the nuclear envelope throughout the cell. There are two types of ER: rough and smooth. Rough ER is studded with ribosomes and is responsible for protein synthesis. Smooth ER lacks ribosomes and is involved in lipid synthesis and detoxification.

Golgi Apparatus
The Golgi apparatus is a stack of flattened, membrane-bound sacs that receive, modify, and package proteins and lipids from the ER for transport to their final destinations within and outside of the cell.

Mitochondria
Mitochondria are known as the powerhouses of the cell because they produce the majority of the cell's energy in the form of ATP. They have their own DNA and are believed to have originated from ancient bacteria that were engulfed by ancestral cells.
