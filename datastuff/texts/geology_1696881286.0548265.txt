Geological Time Scale

The geological time scale is a method of organizing and understanding the history of Earth. It is divided into different units of time, each representing a significant period of geological events and changes.

The largest units of time in the geological time scale are eons, which are divided into eras, then periods, and finally epochs. The four eons are the Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic, and Phanerozoic. The Phanerozoic eon is further divided into three eras: Paleozoic, Mesozoic, and Cenozoic. Each era is then divided into several periods, and each period is further divided into epochs.

One of the most important events in the geological time scale is the Cambrian explosion, which marks the beginning of the Phanerozoic eon and the diversification of life on Earth. This event occurred approximately 541 million years ago and is characterized by a rapid increase in the variety and complexity of animal life.

The geological time scale is also used to classify rocks and fossils. For example, fossils found in sedimentary rocks from the Jurassic period are classified as Jurassic fossils, indicating that they are approximately 201 to 145 million years old.
