Content: On this page, we will be discussing the structure and function of viruses. As you may already know, viruses are microscopic infectious agents that can cause diseases in plants, animals, and humans. They are much smaller than bacteria and cannot reproduce on their own, instead relying on host cells to replicate.

The structure of a virus is composed of genetic material, either DNA or RNA, surrounded by a protein coat called a capsid. Some viruses also have an outer envelope made of lipids taken from the host cell. The shape of the virus can vary greatly, from spherical to rod-shaped to even complex structures.

One of the key characteristics of viruses is their ability to infect host cells. This process begins with the attachment of the virus to a specific receptor on the host cell, which is determined by the shape and structure of the virus. Once attached, the virus injects its genetic material into the host cell and takes over its machinery to produce more viral particles.

Viruses can cause a range of diseases, from the common cold to more severe illnesses like AIDS and Ebola. They can also have a significant impact on agriculture, causing diseases in crops and livestock.
